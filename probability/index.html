Probability Exercises

Here is a list of 10 very cool exercises in probability theory. If you could work out all of them then you are almost guaranteed to have a decent grasp on the basics of probability and martingale theory. No knowledge of stochastic calculus and Brownian motion (beyond its definition) is needed. Most of them are not supposed to be trivial, but they should not be impossible either especially with the hints I have given. Feel free to contact me if you would like to discuss solutions and I would be happy to assist!

  1. For $f,g\in L^1(\mathbb{R})$, show that $f\ast g \in L^1(\mathbb{R})$ where $f \ast g : \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ is the convolution of $f$ and $g$ defined by $$ (f\ast g)(x) = \int_\mathbb{R} f(x-t) g(t)\,dt.$$ Now using the fact that $f\ast g$ is continuous whenever $f\in L^1(\mathbb{R})$ and $g:\mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{R}$ is bounded and measurable, prove that if $A,B$ are measurable sets with $\lambda(A) >0$ and $\lambda(B)>0$ then there exists an open interval $I\subset\mathbb{R}$ such that $$I \subset A + B := \{a + b : a \in A, b \in B\}$$

  2. Let $X_1,X_2,\ldots$ be an iid sequence with $\mathbb{P}(X_1 \in [m,M])=1$ for some $0<m<M<\infty$. Show that $\mathbb{E}(X_2 / X_1) < \infty$ and that $$\frac{1}{n} \left( \frac{X_2}{X_1} + \frac{X_3}{X_2}+\cdots+\frac{X_{n+1}}{X_n} \right) \to \mathbb{E}\left(\frac{X_2}{X_1}\right) $$ almost surely as $n\to\infty$.

  3. Let $X, Y$ be two independent real-valued random variables defined on $(\Omega,\mathcal{F},\mathbb{P}).$ Let $f : \mathbb{R}^2\to\mathbb{R}$ be a bounded Borel function. Show that $\mathbb{E}[f(X,Y)|Y] = \mathbb{E}(f(X,y))|_{y=Y}.$ Hint: first show the result holds for indicator functions $f = 1_\Gamma$ where $\Gamma \in \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}^2)$; you may wish to use the $\pi$-$\lambda$ theorem for this.

  4. Let $X_1,X_2,\ldots$ be a sequence of independent random variables such that $$ X_n = \begin{cases} n^2 - 1 & \text{with probability } n^{-2}\\ -1 & \text{with probability } 1-n^{-2} \end{cases}$$ Show that for every $n$ we have $\mathbb{E}(X_n) = 0$ but almost surely we have $$\frac{X_1+X_2+\cdots+X_n}{n} \to -1 \quad \text{as }n\to\infty.$$ Thus, the central limit theorem fails to hold for non-identically distributed sequences with the same mean.

  5. In this question you are asked to prove the Kolmogorov 0-1 law using martingales. Let $(\Omega,\mathcal{F},\mathbb{P})$ be a probability space.

    (a) Let $(\mathcal{F}_n)$ be a filtration such that $\mathcal{F}=\bigcup_n \mathcal{F}_n$. Prove that for all $f\in L^1(\Omega)$ we have $\mathbb{E}(f|\mathcal{F}_n)\to f$ almost surely as $n\to\infty$.

    (b) Let $(X_n)$ be independent $L^1(\Omega)$ random variables. Define for all $n$, $\mathcal{F}_n = \sigma(X_j : j=0,\ldots,n)$ and $\mathcal{T}_n =\sigma(X_j : j\geq n).$ Assume that $\mathcal{F} = \bigcup_n \mathcal{F}_n$. Consider the tail $\sigma$-algebra $\mathcal{T} = \bigcap_n \mathcal{T}_n.$ Prove that for all $C\in\mathcal{T}$ and $n$, we have that $$\mathbb{E}(1_C | \mathcal{F}_n)=\mathbb{P}(C).$$ Deduce that $\mathbb{P}(C)\in\{0, 1\}.$

  6. Let $\xi_1,\xi_2,\ldots$ be iid random variables defined on a probability space $(\Omega,\mathcal{F},\mathbb{P})$ such that $\mathbb{P}(\xi_k = 1)=\mathbb{P}(\xi_k=-1)=\frac{1}{2}$ for all $k.$ Let $\mathcal{F}_n = \sigma(\xi_1,\ldots,\xi_n)$, and let $S_n = \sum_{k=1}^n \xi_k.$ For $r\in\mathbb{R}$, define $$M_n(r)=\exp\left(rS_n-\frac{nr^2}{2}\right),\quad n\geq 1.$$

    (a) Show that for every $r\in\mathbb{R}$ the process $(M_n(r))$ is an $(\mathcal{F}_n)$-supermartingale.

    (b) Let $H_n = \int_\mathbb{R} M_n(r) \,dr.$ Show that $(H_n)$ is an $(\mathcal{F}_n)$-supermartingale and that $$H_n=\sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{n}}e^{S_n^2/2n}.$$

    (c) Show that $H_\infty := \lim_{n\to\infty} H_n$ exists almost surely and deduce that $$\sup_{n\geq 1}\frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}e^{S_n^2/2n} < \infty \quad \text{a.s.}$$

    (d) Hence prove that $$ \limsup_{n\to\infty} \frac{|S_n|}{\sqrt{2n\log n}} \leq 1 \quad \text{a.s.} $$

  7. Let $(B_t)_{t\geq 0}$ be a Brownian motion defined on $(\Omega,\mathcal{F},\mathbb{P})$, $\sigma : \mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{R}$ be bounded and Lipschitz continuous, and $x_0\in L^2(\Omega).$ Let $(X_n)_{n=1}^\infty$ and $(Y_n)_{n=1}^\infty$ be processes with $X_j,Y_j\in L^2(\Omega)$ for all $j$ such that they solve the stochastic difference equation $$\begin{cases} Z_{n+1}=Z_{n}+\sigma(Z_{n})(B_{n+1}-B_{n}) & n=1,2,\ldots\\ Z_{0}=x_{0} \end{cases} $$ Prove that $X_j = Y_j$ almost surely for all $j$.

  8. In this question you are asked to prove Rademacher’s theorem (locally Lipschitz functions are differentiable a.e.) using martingales. Consider the probability space $(\Omega,\mathcal{F},\mathbb{P}) = ([0,1),\mathcal{B}([0,1)), \lambda).$ Let $A_{k,n} = [\frac{k-1}{2^n}, \frac{k}{2^n})$ for $k=1,\ldots,2^n$ and $n=0,1,\ldots$

    (a) Let $\mathcal{F}_n := \sigma(A_{k,n} : k\leq 2^n).$ Show that $\mathcal{F} = \sigma(\bigcup_{n=0}^\infty \mathcal{F}_n).$

    (b) For $n\geq 0$ let $\xi_n(\omega) := \sum_{k=1}^{2^n}\frac{k-1}{2^n} 1_{A_{k,n}}(\omega)$ and let $f :[0,1]\to \mathbb{R}$ be a bounded measurable function. Let $$X_n(\omega) := \frac{f(\xi_n(\omega)+2^{-n})-f(\xi_n(\omega))}{2^{-n}}.$$ Show that $(X_n)$ is an $(\mathcal{F}_n)$-martingale.

    (c) Assume now that $f:[0,1]\to\mathbb{R}$ is Lipschitz continuous. Show that $X_\infty := \lim_{n\to\infty}X_n$ exists almost surely.

    (d) Show that $f(x)-f(0)=\int^x_0 X_\infty(\omega)\,d\omega$ for all $x\in[0,1].$ Conclude that $f$ is differentiable almost everywhere.

  9. In this question you are asked to give a proof of the Lebesgue differentiation theorem using martingales. Recall that the Lebesgue differentiation theorem says that for any locally integrable function $f:\mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{R}$ we have $$ \lim_{\tiny\stackrel{\lambda(B)\to0}{B \ni x}}\frac{1}{\lambda(B)} \int_B f(y)\,dy = f(x) \quad \text{for almost every }x\in\mathbb{R} $$ where the limit is taken over open balls $B$ containing $x$ as $\lambda(B)$ approaches $0.$ Consider $(\Omega,\mathcal{F},\mathbb{P}) = ([0,1),\mathcal{B}([0,1)), \lambda)$ with $A_{k,n}$ and $\mathcal{F}_n$ defined as in the previous question. Let $f\in L^1([0,1))$ and define $M_k(f) := \mathbb{E}(f|\mathcal{F}_k).$

    (a) Show that $(M_k(f))_{k=0}^\infty$ is an $(\mathcal{F}_n)$-martingale and show that it converges almost surely to some $M_\infty(f) \in L^1([0,1)).$

    (b) Show that for any $k=1,2,\ldots$, $M_k(f)$ can be expressed as $$M_k(f)(\omega) = \frac{1}{\lambda(I_\omega)}\int_{I_\omega}f(s)\,ds\quad\forall \omega\in [0,1)$$ where $I_\omega$ is the unique interval in $\{ A_{m,k} : 1 \leq m \leq 2^{k} \}$ containing $\omega.$ Hint: you are essentially asked to show that $M_k(f) = \sum_{i=0}^{2^k-1} 1_{[i2^{-k}, (i+1)2^{-k})}2^k \int^{(i+1)2^{-k}}_{i2^{-k}}f(s)\,ds.$

    (c) Show that for any $g\in C([0,1])$, $M_\infty(g)=g.$ Hint: use the uniform continuity of $g.$

    (d) Prove that $M_\infty(f)=f$ almost surely. Conclude that the Lebesgue differentiation theorem holds. Hint: it suffices to show that for every $\epsilon > 0$ it holds that $\mathbb{P}(\lim_{k\to\infty}|M_k(f)-f| > \epsilon)=0$; in doing so you may find the estimate $$|M_k(f)-f| \leq |M_k(f)-M_k(g)|+|M_k(g)-g|+|g-f|$$ useful.

  10. In this question we prove a concentration inequality concerning the modulus of continuity of the Brownian motion. Let $(B_t)_{t\geq 0}$ be a Brownian motion defined on $(\Omega,\mathcal{F},\mathbb{P}).$

    (a) Show that for any $0\leq s < t \leq1,$ $$\mathbb{P}\left( \sup_{s\leq u \leq t} |B_u-B_s|\geq a\sqrt{t-s} \right)\leq 2e^{-a^2/2}.$$ You may find it useful to use the fact that for a standard normal random variable $Z$, $\mathbb{P}(Z \geq z) \leq \frac{1}{2}e^{-z^2/2}$ for all $z >0.$

    (b) Show that for any $n\geq 0,$ $$\mathbb{P}\left(\sup_{k=1,\ldots,2^n}|B_{k/2^n}-B_{(k-1)/2^n}| \geq a2^{-n/2} \right) \leq 2^n e^{-a^2/2}.$$

    (c) Define the modulus of continuity $\mathrm{mod} : [0,1] \to \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}$ by $$ \mathrm{mod}(\delta):=\sup\{ |B_t-B_s| : s,t\in[0,1],\, |s-t|<\delta \}. $$ For a fixed $n\geq 1$, prove that for any $c>\sqrt{18},$ $$\mathbb{P}(\mathrm{mod}(2^{-n} > c\sqrt{2^{-n}\log(2^n)})\leq 3(2^n)^{1-c^2/18}.$$

    (d) Hence show that for any $C>6$ there are positive constants $C_1,C_2$ depending only on $C$ such that for all $\delta$ small enough, $$ \mathbb{P}(\mathrm{mod}(\delta) > C\sqrt{\delta\log(1/\delta)}) \leq C_1\delta^{C_2}. $$ Hint: for $\delta$ small consider $n=\lfloor \log(1/\delta)/\log(2)\rfloor$ and note that it satisfies $2^{-(n+1)}<\delta \leq 2^{-n}$
